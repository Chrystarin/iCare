The Hourglass of Extinction

Dianne Chrystalin Brandez

For thousands of years all kinds of species have walked the face of this earth. Out of all the species that has lived and strived on earth since life began, around 99% has already gone extinct. As of today, earth has gone through five major extinctions, and based on studies and research, we are currently living through the sixth. What makes it worse is that our current rate of extinction is brought about by our own human hands. Based on research, this current extinction that we are facing is much worse than all previous ones due to the much faster rate of species decline.

Humans are, indeed, the most intellectual creatures that roamed the earth, which is not a surprise given that our intellect has brought us the most achievements and advancements throughout all species in this world’s history. But despite all the progress, natural resources are being depleted day by day in exchange for progress, success, and innovation. Trees are cut down for lumber and supplies, mountains are dug up for gems and minerals, animals are being hunted for their exotic parts, and for our exotic tastes, we veer away from ordinary livestock and satisfy our palates with the flavors of the “rare” and “limited”. For a species that already has everything, humans still hunger for more.

Imagine an hourglass that is as heavy as the earth, its top vial filled with a million of singular grains of sand slowly dripping down to the lower chamber. Picture that every grain of sand is a family of birds, a herd of elephants, or even a swarm of butterflies that is slowly ticking away as they live helplessly in a world we built as helpless. We should be more responsible because extinction is something that we can never take back, as with an hourglass that cannot be flipped. Once it happens, once a species has gone extinct, there is no more turning back, and they are lost forever. It is not too late to bring a change to the world and save our biodiversity, because in the face of extinction, we are all at risk.
